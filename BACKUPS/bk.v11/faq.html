Which version of Python is used in Pythons and Ladders?

Pythons and Ladders makes use of the Python emulator from Skulpt.org, whose currently known supported version is Python 2.6+ (2016).

How can I install and set up Python on my computer?

You don't need to; all puzzles can be completed in-browser! However, if you're looking to go the extra mile, here's a link which guides you through the setup process. As far as editors go, we recommend Wing IDE 101 if you've never coded before, and in all other cases, Sublime Text.

When can I expect to complete Pythons and Ladders?

For a beginner, the completion time is intended to be between a week to two months. This, of course, is proportional to individual activity and engagement levels.

How difficult is programming?

Learning to code isn't particularly easy. Compare it to learning another language, like Spanish. Even though most of the alphabet is the same and there are plenty of overlapping words, a native English speaker will still need to put in a considerable amount of effort to be able to communicate in Spanish.

Is Pythons and Ladders a substitute for formal education?

In short, no. For the sake of comparison, Pythons and Ladders covers roughly the same amount of material as the first four weeks of a first-year university course. The same material is presented here in a more self-paced and tiered manner, reducing the slope of the introductory learning curve, and not the curve itself.

How does Pythons and Ladders differ from other e-Learning tools?

Fundamentally, Pythons and Ladders is a DIY approach to coding, falling under the broad umbrella of e-Learning platforms.

When conceiving the original concept, we debated the necessity of developing this resource when so many others existed. Drawing from conversations and research over the following months, we eventually compiled a list of issues common in alternate services:

  • Lack of focus on problem-solving methods, search engine querying skills, and programming as it relates to everyday life
  • Developed or taught by enthusiasts/academics unfamiliar with industry practices, resulting in a learning curve far too steep for absolute beginners
  • Overloaded interfaces, neglecting mobile support and optimizations for slower connections
  • Quality content available only for a fee/subscription, with the "free" or "demo" packages offering inconclusive material

With these insights, we kicked Pythons and Ladders into gear, working on a design intended to address them.

As developers, we understand that not everyone shares the same learning style.

In the interest of competition-free education, here are some similar, well-executed resources you may find useful:

LearnPython.org — Uses a similar interactive & in-browser construct, and is great for reference.

Learn Python the Hard Way — In our opinion, the perfect sequel to Pythons and Ladders.

SoloLearn — A more standardized approach to coding, complete with lessons and quizzes.

Codeacademy — A course-based offering, with plenty of options for learning other languages as well.

Code School — A game-themed approach with pricing plans for personal and business use.
